Gravity & fields

Newton's law of universal gravitation states that every pair of masses attracts each other with a force proportional to each mass and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. This is the underlying law. The familiar \(F_g = mg\) is just a special case near Earth's surface.

\[\left|\vec{F}_g\right| = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\]

Variables

\(G\) \(6.67 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{kg}^2\)
\(m_1,\,m_2\) masses of the two objects (kg)
\(r\) center-to-center distance (m)
Direction attractive, along the line joining both centers

Scaling relationships

\(r\) doubles \(F_g\) decreases by factor of 4
\(r\) halves \(F_g\) increases by factor of 4
Either mass doubles \(F_g\) doubles
3rd law check force on \(m_1\) equals force on \(m_2\) in magnitude

A gravitational field is a vector field that describes the gravitational force per unit mass at every point in space around a source mass \(M\). It lets you treat gravity as a property of space itself rather than as action at a distance.

\[\vec{g} = \frac{\vec{F}_g}{m} = -G\frac{M}{r^2}\hat{r}\]

Gravitational field strength

Definition force per unit mass: \(\vec{g} = \vec{F}_g / m\)
Units \(\text{N/kg} = \text{m/s}^2\)
Direction toward the source mass \(M\)
At Earth's surface \(g = GM_E / R_E^2 \approx 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\)
Far from surface \(g\) decreases as \(1/r^2\)
Using the field \(\vec{F}_g = m\vec{g}\), works for any test mass \(m\)

Universal vs. surface gravity

Surface approximation \(F_g = mg\), valid when \(r \approx R_E\)
Universal law \(F_g = Gm_1m_2/r^2\), exact, works everywhere
When to use universal law orbits, altitude problems, non-Earth bodies

Inclines

On a horizontal surface, \(\vec{F}_g\) and \(\vec{F}_n\) are equal and opposite, so the block doesn't accelerate. When the surface is tilted to angle \(\theta\), \(\vec{F}_n\) remains perpendicular to the surface but is no longer aligned with \(\vec{F}_g\), which still pulls straight down. The forces become unbalanced and the block accelerates.

Horizontal surface

\(F_g\) \(= mg\) (downward)
\(F_n\) \(= mg\) (upward, balanced)
Net force zero, no acceleration

Inclined surface (angle \(\theta\))

\(F_g\) \(= mg\) (still straight down)
\(F_n\) \(< mg\) (perpendicular to surface)
Net force nonzero, accelerates down the incline

A convenient coordinate system for incline problems orients one axis parallel (\(\parallel\)) to the surface and one axis perpendicular (\(\perp\)) to the surface. In this frame the block only accelerates in one dimension (\(+\!\parallel\), down the slope), simplifying the analysis.

\(\vec{F}_n\) lies entirely in the \(\perp\) dimension. \(\vec{F}_g\) is diagonal to both axes, so it must be resolved into components. The component triangle formed by \(\vec{F}_g\) and its components is similar to the triangle formed by the incline itself, so the same angle \(\theta\) appears in both triangles.

Gravity decomposition on an incline

\[F_{g\,\parallel} = mg\sin\theta \qquad F_{g\,\perp} = mg\cos\theta\]

Forces on the incline

Gravity (parallel) \(mg\sin\theta\), down the slope
Gravity (perpendicular) \(mg\cos\theta\), into the surface
Normal force \(F_n = mg\cos\theta\), balanced by \(F_{g\,\perp}\)
Net force (frictionless) \(mg\sin\theta\), down the slope (unbalanced)

Applying Newton's 2nd law along the slope gives the block's acceleration. You can check the limiting cases: at \(\theta = 0°\) the surface is flat and \(a = 0\); at \(\theta = 90°\) the block is in free fall and \(a = g\).

\[a = g\sin\theta \quad \text{(frictionless incline)}\]

When friction is present and the block is at rest or sliding down, \(\vec{F}_f\) points up the slope (opposing motion). This reduces the net force and therefore the acceleration.

\[a = g\sin\theta - \frac{F_f}{m} \quad \text{(with friction, sliding down)}\]

Key reminders

Memorize \(F_{g\,\parallel} = mg\sin\theta\), \(F_{g\,\perp} = mg\cos\theta\)
Perpendicular direction always balanced (no acceleration \(\perp\) to surface)
Friction direction up slope if sliding/stationary down; down slope if sliding up
Sanity check test \(\theta = 0°\) and \(\theta = 90°\) to verify your expression

Friction

Block on an incline
adjust µs and µk to explore static vs kinetic friction

When two surfaces are in contact and sliding (or something attempts to make them slide), interatomic electric forces produce a macroscopic force component parallel to the surfaces called friction. The perpendicular component is the normal force. Friction depends on only two factors: the normal force between the surfaces and the material properties of the surfaces in contact. It does not depend on sliding speed or contact area.

Kinetic friction

When surfaces sliding relative to each other
Direction opposes sliding
Formula \(F_{f,k} = \mu_k F_n\)
Magnitude constant for given \(F_n\)

Static friction

When surfaces not sliding, prevents motion
Direction opposes attempted sliding
Formula \(F_{f,s} \leq \mu_s F_n\)
Magnitude adjusts up to a max

Kinetic friction is always equal to \(\mu_k F_n\), while static friction can be anything from zero up to \(\mu_s F_n\). The actual static friction force depends on the other forces acting. It adopts whatever value is needed to prevent sliding, until the applied force exceeds the maximum.

\[F_{f,s\,\text{max}} = \mu_s F_n \qquad F_{f,k} = \mu_k F_n\]

What affects friction magnitude?

Normal force yes, directly proportional
Material properties yes, determines \(\mu\)
Sliding speed no
Contact area no

Why do these factors matter? All surfaces have microscopic imperfections ("hills" and "valleys"). The normal force determines how tightly the surfaces are pressed together, pushing the imperfections deeper into each other and making sliding harder. The material properties determine the shape and density of these imperfections.

Coefficient of friction (\(\mu\))

Units dimensionless (N/N cancel)
Typical range between 0 and 1
\(\mu_s\) vs. \(\mu_k\) typically \(\mu_s > \mu_k\) for any surface pair
Implication harder to start sliding than to keep sliding

Common \(\mu_k\) values

Rubber on asphalt \(0.5 - 0.8\)
Rubber on ice \(0.15\)
Wood on wood \(0.30\)
Steel on steel \(0.57\)
Teflon on teflon \(0.04\)

The general model for friction combines both types into one expression. For kinetic friction, use \(=\) with \(\mu_k\). For static friction, use \(\leq\) with \(\mu_s\), then use Newton's 2nd law to find the actual value.

\[\left|\vec{F}_f\right| \leq \mu\left|\vec{F}_n\right|\]

Friction on an incline

Normal force on incline \(F_n = mg\cos\theta\)
Kinetic friction on incline \(F_{f,k} = \mu_k mg\cos\theta\)
Acceleration (sliding down) \(a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)\)
Stationary on incline when \(\tan\theta \leq \mu_s\)

Spring force

Mass hanging from a spring

Springs exert a force that depends on how much their length is changed from their equilibrium length \(L_0\), the length at which the spring exerts no force. The farther a spring is stretched or compressed from equilibrium, the stronger the force it exerts.

Experiments show that the spring force \(\vec{F}_s\) is directly proportional to the displacement \(\Delta\vec{x}\) from equilibrium, and always points opposite to the displacement, always back toward equilibrium. This relationship is called Hooke's law.

\[\vec{F}_s = -k\,\Delta\vec{x}\]

Variables

\(\vec{F}_s\) force the spring exerts (N)
\(k\) spring constant (N/m), always positive
\(\Delta\vec{x}\) displacement from equilibrium (m)
Negative sign force opposes displacement

The spring constant \(k\)

Meaning stiffness of the spring
Large \(k\) stiff spring, more force per unit stretch
Small \(k\) loose spring, less force per unit stretch
Units \(\text{N/m}\)

Restoring force behavior

At equilibrium (\(\Delta x = 0\)) spring exerts zero force
Stretched (\(\Delta x > 0\)) \(F_s\) is negative, pulls back
Compressed (\(\Delta x < 0\)) \(F_s\) is positive, pushes back
Direction always toward equilibrium

On a graph of \(F_s\) vs. \(\Delta x\), Hooke's law produces a straight line through the origin with slope \(-k\). A stiffer spring has a steeper slope. The spring constant can be determined experimentally from this slope.

Ideal spring assumptions

Mass negligible, no inertia or weight
Hooke's law followed perfectly at all displacements
Real springs deviate at large displacements
Permanent deformation occurs if stretched/compressed too far
