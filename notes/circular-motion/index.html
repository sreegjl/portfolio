Centripetal acceleration

Top view: block on a frictionless table, string to center
\(F_g\) and \(F_n\) cancel, tension provides centripetal force

When a net force acts perpendicular to an object's velocity, the object changes direction without changing speed. If this perpendicular force always points toward a fixed center, the object follows a circular path. The inward acceleration is called centripetal acceleration, and the net inward force causing it is called centripetal force.

\[a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} \qquad \vec{a}_c = \frac{\sum \vec{F}_{\text{rad}}}{m}\]

Directions

Velocity \(\vec{v}\) tangent to the circular path
\(\sum \vec{F}_{\text{rad}}\) toward the center (radial)
\(\vec{a}_c\) toward the center (radial)

Scaling

\(v\) doubles \(a_c\) quadruples
\(r\) doubles \(a_c\) halves
\(m\) doubles, same \(a_c\) \(\sum F_{\text{rad}}\) doubles

"Centripetal force" is not a new type of force. It's just the net radial force from whatever forces point toward or away from the center (tension, gravity, normal force, friction, etc.). When multiple forces have radial components, add them as vectors to find \(\sum \vec{F}_{\text{rad}}\).

Vertical circles

Ball swung in a vertical circle
Both \(F_T\) and \(F_g\) point toward center

At the top

Radial forces \(F_T\) and \(F_g\) both point inward
\(\sum F_{\text{rad}}\) \(F_T + mg\)
Min speed to maintain path \(v_{\text{min}} = \sqrt{gr}\) (when \(F_T = 0\))

At angle \(\theta\) from bottom

Radial component of \(F_g\) \(mg\cos\theta\) (outward)
Tangential component of \(F_g\) \(mg\sin\theta\)
\(\sum F_{\text{rad}}\) \(F_T - mg\cos\theta\)
\(\sum F_{\text{tan}}\) \(mg\sin\theta\)

An object in circular motion moves at constant speed only if there is zero tangential force. A nonzero tangential force causes the object to speed up or slow down along its path. The net acceleration is the vector sum of \(\vec{a}_c\) (radial) and \(\vec{a}_{\text{tan}}\) (tangential).

Key reminders

Applies to partial arcs too a car rounding a curve is circular motion
Constant speed only radial accel, no tangential force
Changing speed both radial and tangential accel
Equate \(a_c\) expressions \(\dfrac{v^2}{r} = \dfrac{\sum F_{\text{rad}}}{m}\)

Orbits & Kepler's law

Orbital simulator

An object in uniform circular motion follows a circular path at constant speed. Its period \(T\) is the time for one full revolution, and its frequency \(f\) is cycles per unit time. They are inverses: \(T = 1/f\).

\[T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} \qquad f = \frac{1}{T}\]

Period and frequency

\(T\) (period) time per revolution (s)
\(f\) (frequency) \(= 1/T\), revolutions per second
Speed from orbit \(v = 2\pi r / T\)

Satellite orbits

Centripetal force source gravity: \(F_g = G\dfrac{mM}{r^2}\)
Orbital acceleration \(a = G\dfrac{M}{r^2}\)
Orbital speed \(v^2 = GM/r\)

For a satellite in a circular orbit, gravity provides the centripetal force. Setting \(v^2/r = GM/r^2\) and substituting \(v = 2\pi r/T\) gives Kepler's third law: orbital period squared is proportional to orbital radius cubed.

\[T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}\,r^3 \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad T^2 \propto r^3\]

Key facts

Acceleration depends on \(M\) and \(r\) only (not satellite mass)
Comparing orbits around same body \(T^2 \propto r^3\) since \(4\pi^2/GM\) is constant
Real orbits elliptical, but often nearly circular
Assumption central body much more massive than satellite
