Newton's laws of motion

Three laws describe how forces govern motion: the 1st law says objects resist changes to their motion, the 2nd law relates net force to acceleration, and the 3rd law describes how objects interact through paired forces.

The three laws

1st law no net force \(\Rightarrow\) constant velocity
2nd law \(\displaystyle \sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}\)
3rd law \(\vec{F}_{\text{A on B}} = -\vec{F}_{\text{B on A}}\)

Common forces

Weight \(F_g = mg\)
Normal force \(N\), \(\perp\) to surface
Friction \(f = \mu N\)
Tension \(T\), equal along an ideal rope
Applied force \(F_{\text{app}}\)
Person on a scale in an elevator

The 1st law states that an object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted on by a net external force. So a system at rest or moving at constant velocity is in translational equilibrium, meaning its acceleration is zero and the net external force must also be zero.

\[\sum \vec{F} = 0 \iff \vec{a} = 0\]

Acceleration vs. velocity direction

Direction of \(\vec{a}\) always matches the direction of \(\sum \vec{F}\)
\(\vec{a}\) same direction as \(\vec{v}\) system speeds up
\(\vec{a}\) opposite direction to \(\vec{v}\) system slows down
Inertial reference frame non-accelerating frame where Newton's laws hold

The 2nd law states that the acceleration of a system is directly proportional to the net force acting on it, inversely proportional to its mass, and in the same direction as the net force. So \(\vec{a}\) is directly proportional to \(\sum \vec{F}\) and inversely proportional to \(m\). The larger the mass, the more force it takes to produce the same acceleration.

\[\vec{a} = \frac{\sum \vec{F}}{m}\]

Force, mass & acceleration

\(\sum \vec{F}\) doubles, \(m\) constant \(\vec{a}\) doubles
\(m\) doubles, \(\sum \vec{F}\) constant \(\vec{a}\) halves
Units \(1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2\)
Mass vs. weight mass is constant; weight (\(F_g = mg\)) depends on \(g\)

The 3rd law states that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces never exist alone. They arise from interactions between two objects, so they always come in pairs. If object \(\text{A}\) exerts a force on object \(\text{B}\), then \(\text{B}\) exerts a force back on \(\text{A}\) that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

\[\vec{F}_{\text{A on B}} = -\vec{F}_{\text{B on A}}\]

Force pair facts

Act on two different objects
Magnitude always equal
Direction always opposite
Force type always the same (both gravity, both contact, etc.)
Net effect of a pair zero (but on different objects)

Internal vs. external forces

A system is any object or group of objects you choose to analyze together. Once a system is defined, every force acting on it is either internal (both members of the pair act within the system) or external (only one member acts on the system).

Internal forces

Pair members both act inside the system
Net effect on system zero, they cancel
Shown on system's FBD? no

External forces

Pair members other half acts outside the system
Net effect on system can change its motion
Shown on system's FBD? yes

Choosing a system

Smaller system more interactions become external
Larger system more interactions become internal (cancel)
Throughout a problem stay consistent with one chosen system

Forces in 2D

The \(x\) and \(y\) directions are independent: a system can be balanced (non-accelerating) along one axis while unbalanced (accelerating) along the other.

Resolving forces by axis

Net force, \(x\)-axis \(\displaystyle \sum F_x = ma_x\)
Net force, \(y\)-axis \(\displaystyle \sum F_y = ma_y\)
Force at angle \(\theta\), components \(F_x = F\cos\theta\), \(\;F_y = F\sin\theta\)
\(\sum F_x = 0\) but \(\sum F_y \neq 0\) accelerates only along \(y\)

Free-body diagrams

Once internal forces are accounted for, a system can be drawn as a single point, regardless of how many objects it contains. Only external forces appear on its free-body diagram.

Drawing a system FBD

1 Represent the entire system as a single dot
2 Only draw forces from objects outside the system
3 Leave off internal force pairs (they cancel)
4 Apply \(\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}\) using the system's total mass
Box pushed across a surface
\(\sum F = 20\text{ N right}\), \(a = 4\text{ m/s}^2\)

Center of mass

The center of mass is the point where a system's mass can be treated as concentrated. It's where gravity effectively acts, and the point represented by the dot in a system's FBD.

\[x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum_i m_i x_i}{\sum_i m_i}\]

Two-object system

General formula \(\displaystyle x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2}{m_1 + m_2}\)
Equal masses midpoint between them
Unequal masses closer to the heavier object

Finding the center of mass

Look for lines of symmetry (CM lies on them)
Adding mass off-center shifts CM toward the added mass
Gravity acts at the system's center of mass

Problem solving

Steps

1 Define the system, whether a single object or a group
2 Identify only the external forces acting on it, then draw the FBD
3 Leave internal forces off the diagram (they cancel within the system)
4 Apply \(\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}\), or \(\sum \vec{F} = 0\) for constant velocity / rest
5 If forces act at angles, resolve them into \(x\)- and \(y\)-components first
6 Solve \(\sum F_x = ma_x\) and \(\sum F_y = ma_y\) separately since the axes are independent
7 For questions about internal structure (e.g. center of mass), analyze mass distribution separately
