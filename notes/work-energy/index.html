Work

Thruster force on a spacecraft, single instant

The energy transferred to or from an object by a force is called work. The work done by an individual force on an object during a displacement is:

\[W = Fd\cos\theta\]

Variables

\(F\) magnitude of the individual force (N)
\(d\) magnitude of the displacement (m)
\(\theta\) angle between force and displacement vectors
Units joules (J)

Sign of work

\(\theta = 0°\) (parallel) \(W = +Fd\)
\(\theta = 90°\) (perpendicular) \(W = 0\)
\(\theta = 180°\) (antiparallel) \(W = -Fd\)

Work is a scalar: only the component of force along the displacement direction does work, \(\cos\theta\) extracts it. A force perpendicular to displacement, like gravity on a satellite in circular orbit, does zero work. Try the angle slider above to see how tilting the thrust changes it.

Since \(W = Fd\) for a constant force, work is just the area between the force curve and the d-axis: a rectangle when force is constant, a trapezoid when it varies linearly. Try the sliders below.

Kinetic energy

When an object is in translational motion, modeled as a single point at its center of mass, it has translational kinetic energy depending on its mass and speed:

\[\class{var-k}{K} = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2\]

Key properties

Units joules \((\text{J}) = \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 / \text{s}^2\)
Type scalar,magnitude only, no direction
Minimum value \(K \geq 0\) always

The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy, the work-kinetic energy theorem:

\[W_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} W_i = \Delta \class{var-k}{K} = \class{var-k}{K_f} - \class{var-k}{K_i}\]

Applying the theorem

Net work > 0 kinetic energy increases,object speeds up
Net work < 0 kinetic energy decreases,object slows down
Net work = 0 kinetic energy unchanged,constant speed
Multiple forces sum work by each, or use \(\vec{F}_{\text{net}}\)
Reference frame \(K\) and \(W_{\text{net}}\) depend on the observer's frame

Potential energy

When a force does negative work on an object, it removes kinetic energy. Whether that energy is recoverable depends on the type of force:

Conservative vs. nonconservative

Conservative (gravity, spring force) negative work stored as potential energy, recoverable
Nonconservative (friction, air resistance) negative work dissipated as heat/sound, not recoverable

Potential energy \((U)\) is energy associated with the configuration, the relative positions, of objects in a system that interact via a conservative force.

Gravity requires a system of two interacting masses, modeled by Newton's law of universal gravitation, \(F_g = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\). The system's gravitational potential energy is:

\[\class{var-u}{U_g} = -G\dfrac{m_1 m_2}{r}\]

Key properties

Sign always negative
As \(r \to \infty\) \(U_g \to 0\)
As \(r\) decreases \(U_g\) becomes more negative

Near Earth's surface, \(g\) is nearly constant and \(F_g = mg\). The corresponding change in potential energy is:

\[\Delta \class{var-u}{U_g} = mg\Delta y\]

Only changes in \(U_g\) matter, the zero reference is arbitrary and chosen for convenience.

The other common conservative force is the spring force. A spring stretched or compressed by \(\Delta x\) from its relaxed length exerts a restoring force, \(F_s = k\Delta x\) (Hooke's law). The elastic potential energy stored is:

\[\class{var-u}{U_s} = \dfrac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2\]

Spring properties

Sign \(U_s \geq 0\) always,depends on \((\Delta x)^2\)
Stretch vs. compression same \(|\Delta x|\) either way stores equal \(U_s\)

Mechanical energy

Pendulum released from rest

Mechanical energy is the sum of a system's total kinetic and potential energy:

\[\class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} = \class{var-k}{K_{\text{tot}}} + \class{var-u}{U_{\text{tot}}}\]

If only conservative forces act, mechanical energy is conserved: kinetic and potential energy trade off, but the total stays fixed. Nonconservative forces like friction have no associated potential energy, the negative work they do is dissipated and lost from the system instead.

Applying conservation

Only conservative forces act \(\Delta \class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} = 0\)
Nonconservative forces act \(\Delta \class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} = W_{\text{nc}}\)

Work done by conservative forces is also path-independent, it depends only on the initial and final configuration, not the route taken. Work done by nonconservative forces like friction is path-dependent.

Conservation of energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed. The total energy of a closed system stays fixed: it can shift between kinetic, potential, and thermal forms, but the sum never changes. When a system is not closed, external forces can transfer energy in or out:

\[W_{\text{ext}} = \Delta \class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} + \Delta E_{\text{th}}\]

\(\Delta E_{\text{th}}\) is the thermal energy generated inside the system by nonconservative forces like friction. It is always positive (heat only flows out of mechanical energy, never back in), so friction always reduces \(E_{\text{mech}}\). The special case \(\Delta \class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} = 0\) from the previous section is just this equation with \(W_{\text{ext}} = 0\) and \(\Delta E_{\text{th}} = 0\).

Applying the general form

No external work, no friction inside \(\Delta \class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} = 0\)
No external work, friction inside \(\Delta \class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} = -\Delta E_{\text{th}}\), mechanical energy lost to heat
External work done on system \(W_{\text{ext}} > 0\) adds energy, \(W_{\text{ext}} < 0\) removes it

The system boundary you choose changes which terms appear. Including the spring and Earth inside lets you account for their forces as \(\Delta U\). Placing them outside means their forces show up as \(W_{\text{ext}}\) instead. Either framing is valid: the same energy is being tracked, just partitioned differently.

Key takeaways

Summary

Kinetic energy \(\class{var-k}{K} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\), scalar, always \(\geq 0\)
Work by a force \(W = Fd\cos\theta\), scalar, can be \(+\), \(-\), or \(0\)
Work-energy theorem \(W_{\text{net}} = \Delta \class{var-k}{K}\)
Perpendicular forces do zero work,change direction, not speed
Constant force assumption if force varies, use calculus or a \(F\) vs. \(d\) graph
Conservative forces store removed kinetic energy as potential energy, recoverable
Mechanical energy \(\class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} = \class{var-k}{K} + \class{var-u}{U}\), conserved if only conservative forces act
General conservation \(W_{\text{ext}} = \Delta \class{var-emech}{E_{\text{mech}}} + \Delta E_{\text{th}}\), energy is never created or destroyed
